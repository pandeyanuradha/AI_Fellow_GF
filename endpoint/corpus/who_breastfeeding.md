# WHO/UNICEF Infant and Young Child Feeding Guidelines

**Sources:**
- WHO/UNICEF Global Strategy for Infant and Young Child Feeding (2003)
- WHO recommendations on exclusive breastfeeding (reaffirmed 2023)
- UNICEF/WHO IYCF guidelines

## Exclusive breastfeeding

WHO and UNICEF recommend **exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months of life**. "Exclusive" means the infant receives only breast milk — no other liquids or solids, not even water — except for oral rehydration solution, vitamins, minerals or medicines.

After 6 months, infants should receive nutritionally adequate and safe **complementary foods** while continuing **breastfeeding for up to 2 years of age or beyond**.

## Why 6 months

Evidence from systematic reviews shows that exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months (compared to 3-4 months) confers important health benefits:
- Reduced risk of gastrointestinal infection
- Reduced risk of respiratory infection
- No deficit in growth among healthy term infants
- Reduced maternal postpartum weight retention

## Early initiation

Breastfeeding should be **initiated within the first hour of life** ("early initiation"). This:
- Provides the infant with colostrum, rich in antibodies
- Helps establish breastfeeding
- Supports uterine contraction and reduces postpartum haemorrhage in the mother
- Helps maintain newborn body temperature through skin-to-skin contact

## Practical guidance

- Feed on demand, day and night (at least 8-12 times per 24 hours initially)
- Avoid pacifiers and bottles, which can confuse the infant's suck
- Both breasts may be offered at each feed; allow the baby to finish one side
- Signs of adequate intake: 6+ wet nappies per day, regular weight gain, alert and active baby

## Common myths

- **Myth:** "Mother needs water for the baby in hot weather." **Fact:** Breast milk is ~88% water and meets all hydration needs in the first 6 months.
- **Myth:** "Colostrum is dirty or thin and should be discarded." **Fact:** Colostrum is the perfect first food — concentrated antibodies, growth factors, and easily digested.

## Continued breastfeeding

- 6-12 months: breast milk remains the primary source of nutrition; complementary foods complement it.
- 12-24 months: breast milk still provides ~1/3 of energy needs and continued immune protection.
